The research

The notes

Each week, one consequential proposal gets a close, law-review-style reading — not a summary, an analysis. Every note is human-reviewed, every source verified against the original, and the AI's role disclosed.

What every note delivers

  1. What it does — the operative change, in plain language, pinpoint-cited to the rule.
  2. Legal authority & durability — the statute the agency relies on, and the rule's exposure under the major-questions doctrine and post-Loper Bright review: will it survive a challenge? the question a feed can't answer
  3. Stakes — who is affected, and the real trade-offs.
  4. The assessment — a clearly-marked view: is this lawful, workable, and wise?
  5. The comment window — the deadline and what's genuinely at issue, while you can still act.

How it works

Method & guardrails

A pipeline with a human in the loop — and a deliberate choice to be slow where it counts. AI does the heavy lifting; a person makes the call before anything is published.

1

Watch

Track the full field from the Federal Register and surface the week's most consequential proposal.

2

Read & analyze

Read the primary sources closely — including whether the agency has the authority and whether the rule will survive review.

3

Verify & review

Check every citation against its source, flag anything unverifiable — then a human reads, edits, and approves.

4

Publish

Only after sign-off does the note go live, with a PDF and an AI-use disclosure.

This is research, not a compliance feed. No alerts, no risk scores, no task lists — and nothing published autonomously. A model can invent plausible-looking cases and citations, and in policy a confident error is worse than an obvious one. So a human reviews and approves every note, every citation is checked against its source, and the AI's role is disclosed. Slower, deeper, accountable to a person. Nothing here is legal advice.
