The Financial Regulation Lab

A one-person research desk that tracks every live U.S. financial-regulation proposal and publishes a weekly, human-reviewed note on what the most consequential ones mean for policy.

By Jacob Gerszten · Independent researcher · drafted with AI, reviewed and approved by a human before anything is published

The regulatory radar

Every live proposal, in one place

Proposed and recently finalized rules from the nine core U.S. financial regulators, pulled straight from the Federal Register. Open comment windows are listed first — these are the proposals you can still weigh in on. Search, or filter by agency.

The notes

Weekly research notes

Each week, the Lab picks one consequential proposal from the radar and publishes a concise, law-review-style note: what it does, the legal authority it rests on, who it affects, and how it fits the broader policy debate. Every note is human-reviewed and carries a full AI-use disclosure.

How it works

Method & guardrails

The Lab is a pipeline with a human in the loop. AI does the heavy lifting; a person makes the call before anything is published.

1

Track

Refresh the radar from the Federal Register and surface the week's most consequential proposals.

2

Draft

Pull the primary sources and draft a note in a consistent law-review style, with every citation tied to retrieved text.

3

Verify & review

Check each citation against its source, flag anything unverifiable — then a human reads, edits, and approves.

4

Publish

Only after sign-off: the note goes live with a PDF, and the scorecard updates.

The approval gate is the point. Language models can invent plausible-looking cases and citations, and in a policy tool a confident error is worse than an obvious one. Nothing here is published autonomously: a human reviews and approves every note, every citation is checked against its source, and each note carries a disclosure of how AI was used. This is a research aid, not legal advice.

About. The Financial Regulation Lab is an independent research project by Jacob Gerszten. The radar's facts come directly from the U.S. Federal Register; the notes are drafted with the assistance of AI and reviewed, edited, and approved by a human before publication. Nothing here is legal advice.
